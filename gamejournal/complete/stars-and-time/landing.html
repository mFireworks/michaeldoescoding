In Stars and Time

Release Date: November 20th, 2023
Genre: Adventure RPG
Developer: insertdisc5
Platform: Playstation 4/5, Nintendo Switch, and PC
Details: Website
Steam

In Stars and Time is a adventure RPG game that came out last year. I hadn’t heard of the game till I saw JelloApocalypse streaming the game. I only watched about 30 minutes of the opening and thought, “this looks like a game I’d enjoy” and decided to stop watching and put the game on my wishlist. Boy am I glad I did, because this game was such a great experience.

Before I get any further with my thoughts of the game, I will say this game is best played going into completely blind. I won’t be mentioning any heavy spoilers within this review, but the game’s concepts that are mentioned on its website are fair game here. For a brief summary, this game is very similar to Undertale, Oneshot, maybe even Earthbound. It’s a very story-heavy game, with lots of hidden bits of lore as you try different things. The gameplay has some interesting mechanics as well, so if that sounds interesting to you, stop reading this and give the game a try. The less you know about the game, the better the experience is.

Story & Worldbuilding

This is where the game shines. The game starts out in a unconventional way by doing a recap of the “current adventure”, as you’re starting the game at the end of the party’s adventure. The King is freezing time in the country of Vaugaurd and has locked himself high above in one of the Houses of the land. Your party has been collecting the orbs to unlock the house he’s within. and put an end to the curse he’s spreading. The party is composed of:

  • Mirabelle, a housemaiden with the power to undo some of the king’s curse.
  • Isabeau, a fighter from a nearby city. He quit his job as a Defender to join the party.
  • Odile, a researcher studying Vaugaurdian culture.
  • Bonnie, a preteen who’s sister has been frozen in time.
  • and you, Siffrin, a wanderer who had nothing better to do.
The game wastes no time in showing you how the party socially interacts with each other, that despite how vast their differences are, they've bonded over their journey. However, that's only the start of it. As mentioned in the game's website, Siffrin is stuck in a time loop. Whenever they die, get frozen in time, or something that prevents them from continuing, they'll restart back to when they wake up in the meadow. This time-looping is where some of the story begins to shine, as you're able to change your interactions a bit and learn more about your party, more about the dangers of the House, making your adventuring easier with each loop. As the game goes on, you learn so much about your party, as they seem to always have something to say while your exploring, which may also change depending on the current circumstances of the story!

... and that's where I have to stop with that. The looping plays more roles into the overall story, but continuing would be be too much information. Outside of the story though, the world around the characters is also very well done. Throughout the game, we'd read a new book and remember "oh! That relates to what that one guy said back then!". Events just don't happen in a vacuum, making the world feel alive. There's whole aspects of the world that are constantly mentioned and referenced, but never are directly answered or involved with the story, showing there's more to this story than the game. Which is so cool! Throughout the game, we'd constantly be coming up with theories of how things are connected, what's going to happen, and so on. I mentioned it to Valerie once, but the game is like a really exciting novel that you just can't put down.

Gameplay

Outside of the story, the core gameplay loop (hehe) feels pretty good. While it's a standard turn-based RPG in terms of combat, the time loop core is integrated into the game design as well. As performing loops is essential towards the progression of the game, it respects your time and doesn't require you to start from the beginning each loop. You can pick different floors to begin from, with or without all the locked doors unlocked, making backtracking a whole lot easier. While your party does lose their levels when you loop, the memories you make with them during the adventure stay and provide them with skills or stat increases. The key thing is, it doesn't feel like your losing progress when you loop time, which is a big positive with any time loop sort of setting.

The combat of the game isn't too deep, as it's fairly standard for a turn-based RPG. It's nothing to really write too much about, but I will say that the fact that it is simple is a benefit. Since you're looping time so much, doing the same fights over and over gets tedious, and if there was no sense of progression in terms of our combat ability, it'd make the game feel like such a slog. At first, the fights are interesting and a little tough, but as the game goes on, they lose their charm and eventually become an annoyance, which fits this game sooo well. Since that's exactly how Siffrin would feel, repeating the same fight over and over turns from a threat, to an annoyance. It's well done, for it's simplicity to merge well with the story, and not drive attention away from the real meat of the game.

Visuals

While I don't have much to say on the visuals, they did a fantastic job, there's one thing I want to mention, as I've only seen it rarely done in other games. The thing that stands out to me is that the game is in a grayscale, which is not a problem at all, the shading that they did is wonderful and still makes it feel colored and fitting. What's interesting though, is that greyscale is apart of the world as well. It comes up in conversation a couple times, but it's just fascinating that they went to lengths to also incorporate the art design into the worldbuilding as well! Just a really cool detail that's worth mentioning.

Overall Thoughts

This game has given me the same feelings that Undertale and Oneshot did on my first playthrough. Not a real gameplay-centric game, but really REALLY delivered on the story and worldbuilding. I'd say it's more akin to an interactive visual novel, where there's gameplay elements to keep you engaged, but the emergent stories that come out of it are due to your own exploring, not where the author decides to take you. Now that we've completed the game, we went back to Jello's video to watch through it, and we saw some new cutscenes because we didn't do the same exact exploration as they did. This game is littered with small details in every nook and cranny, all of which connects the world and party to one another, and it's beautiful. I can't recommend this game enough. I understand it may not be for everyone, but if this is a genre of games that you've enjoyed, then this one will make a lasting impression on you as it has for me.
