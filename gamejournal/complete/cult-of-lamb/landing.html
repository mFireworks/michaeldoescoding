Cult of the Lamb

Release Date: June 10th, 2022
Genre: Rougelike & Town Management
Developer: Massive Monster
Platform: Nintendo Switch, Playstation, Xbox, PC, and Mac
Details: Website

Cult of the Lamb is a Rougelike game developed by Massive Monster. You take on the role of a recently sacrificed sheep that's been reincarnated by a sealed god. With the power of the Red Crown, you travel through the lands of the old faith's bishops taking resources, followers, and lives to grow the power of your own cult. After completing (or failing) a crusade in their lands, you'll spend time in our own cult, cultivating your follower's faith in you. You're able to build various different buildings to fullfil the needs of your followers, and in turn they'll provide you with their faith to strengthen you during your crusades.

I played quite a bit of this when the game initially released, probably about halfway through before I put it down for some reason. Never really came back to it till they announced their local co-op update, as I've been looking for a game for my fiancee and I to play! We had a blast playing it together, building up our cult of follower's named after our friends. It's a nice blend between Rougelike and Base-building aspects, each feeding into each out in a feedback loop.

Gameplay

The core of this game is based off many of the Rouge-like games that have become popular in recent years. You only have a limited amount of health, traveling through randomly generated rooms, with randomly selected weapons and “abilities”. It’s pretty standard in terms of a Rouge-like during a crusade, but your motivation to crusade through the saint’s lands is brought by your own cult’s resources and growth.

Between crusades, you’re spending time at your cult’s home, where your followers worship you and perform various tasks that you can assign to them. What your followers are able to do is tied towards the cult’s development, so if you want them to generate wood, you’ll need to invest resources into making a lumber yard for them to work at. Most resources that you collect will be obtained from your crusades, so the further you progress in your crusades, the more resources you’ll have available to further your cult’s growth.

Growing your cult allows you to unlock more abilities and strength to aid your crusades. As you improve the lives of your followers, they’ll generate devotion and faith, which can be used to unlock more utilities for the cult and abilities for your crusades respectively. It’s a positive feedback loop that gives you a sense of progression. Your cult will grow with your strength, making your crusades easier and progress further, which in turn gives you the resources to further grow your cult.

Overall it’s fun gameplay and feels satisfying to play for the most part. There’s a couple times where the enemy spawns seem unfair, causing us to get damaged before the room fully loads in. The late game feels a bit lacking, as there’s a cap on the amount of growth you can achieve. Once you’ve unlocked all the structures and abilities, your devotion and faith resources just generate the end-game currency, which mainly gives cosmetics and a couple of item unlocks. I would’ve liked the end game to unlock another tree of skills and more structures for the cult, allowing that feeling of progression to last into the post-game story.

Other Aspects

This is a very game-play centric game. The story of the game is fairly minimal, as it’s enough to set the premise as to why you’re crusading against the saints, but it doesn’t play a primary focus in the game. Your followers don’t grow too much as characters, but relations between them can grow and some might reveal that they’re related to each other. There’s a handful of NPCs at some other locations that you can visit, but their interactions are fairly limited and used to introduce other mechanics that their locations have to offer.

There might be more lore to the game than I know, as it seems some of the background lore is locked away as a collectible in the post game, but again, that’s minimal in comparison to the game as a whole. Graphically, the game looks really nice, gives a similar sort of vibe as the Paper Mario games, with the flat looking sprites. The enemy designs vary greatly with each giving district signals when they’re attacking, which helps a lot in learning the timings needed to dodge their attacks.

I don’t really remember the soundtrack very well, it isn’t something I’d listen to on it’s own, but the sound design of the game is great. Landing your attacks and taking damage have distinct sounds so regardless of how chaotic the screen may be, you know when these events happen by the sounds alone. The voices for the NPCs throughout the game also add a bit of charm to them, reminding me a lot of the Animal Crossing voices.

Overall Thoughts

I really enjoy the blend of gameplay that is offered by the game. Going on a crusade feels like it has more purpose than just “get to the end without dying”, as you’re collecting resources to improve your cult, so even if you don’t make it to the end of a crusade, you’ve still progressed forward. It's not as punishing as other Rouge-likes, and has a nice cute charm to it with its presentation. Overall would be a game I'd recommend, especially if you like the dungeon-crawling genre of games.
