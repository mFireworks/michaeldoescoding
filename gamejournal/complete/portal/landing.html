Portal

Release Date: October 10th, 2007
Genre: First-Person Puzzle
Developer: Valve
Platform: Nintendo Switch, Playstation 3, Xbox 360, and PC
Details: Steam

Portal is one of the few games developed by Valve, a relatively short game that experiments with a portal mechanic that was unique for its time. I played this game for the first time years ago, but it’s one of those games that I return to every so often as it’s extremely short but a nice experience.

Gameplay

I’d say the game’s focus is on the gameplay, as the game was originally a tech demonstration for the portal mechanic, which it nails. It’s limiting in that the portals must be placed on surfaces, but with clever thinking it allows the player to get almost anywhere they wish to go. Need to get on a ledge? Place a portal on the ceiling and fall onto it. A door closes too quickly? Shoot a portal through and just walk to the other side. With portals also maintaining your momentum gives them further depth in turning gravity into momentum to launch yourself over gaps with your portal placement.

I think what helps make this mechanic stand out even more is how they present the puzzles to you. Each “test chamber” presents itself as a minimalistic puzzle, only providing you with the exact tools you need to complete it, you just need to figure out how to use the tools. As the game progresses the amount of components within a puzzle increases to advance their complexity. The puzzles have this sterile simplicity to them, but also have depth to them through the means of the portal gun.

Story & Worldbuilding

The main story of the game is short and fairly simple, but the dialogue is what makes it memorable. It's a simple premise of waking up and being instructed to complete various puzzles by a computerized voice, GladOS. Just what she tells you is hilarious at times, as it feels like absurd corporate talk from a company testing on humans. The game is filled with them, but just a sample of one. You enter a room that has a pit filled with acid: "In dangerous testing environments, the Enrichment Center promises to always provide useful advice. For instance, the floor here will kill you. Try to avoid it.".

That's the main draw of the story, there's other lore bits within the game, but those are worth to experience yourself. The premise of putting humans through these test chambers is a simple world, but from the dialogue and setting outside of the chambers tells a story in itself. The dialogue informs you of the culture of the company, at least towards their test subjects, but setting outside breaks that illusion a bit, makes you wonder what happened here. I always liked the concept of the puzzle test chambers, as I would recreate my own chambers in Minecraft for my friends to go through.

Overall Thoughts

This is a fun, short game that you can boot up and finish within an afternoon. Quick and easy to get started, as the puzzles can get tricky as you progress along, ut their simple presentation helps give you that "a-ah" feeling once you do figure it out. I love coming back to it whenever I'm itching for a quick puzzle game to play, and now is making me want to play Portal 2 to re-experience that, as it takes the concepts here, and fleshes them out into a much longer game. This is a classic, and if you have yet to play it and enjoy a good puzzle, I highly recommend it.
